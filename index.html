Before You Begin

Hebrew reads right to left. This is the first mental shift. The first letter — Aleph — appears on the right side of a page. By the end of your studies this will feel completely natural.

📜 Historical Note: Biblical Hebrew is a Semitic language, related to Arabic and Aramaic. It was spoken in ancient Canaan and Israel from roughly 1200 BC onward. The Old Testament was composed over about 1,000 years in this language. It has 22 consonants and reads right to left. Vowel points were added by Jewish scribes called Masoretes in the 6th–10th centuries AD.
💡 Study Tip: Don't try to memorise everything at once. Your brain learns letters through repeated exposure over days. Trust the process — you will meet each letter many times across many lessons.
Part 1 · Overview

The Hebrew Alphabet

The Hebrew alphabet is called the Aleph-Bet (אָלֶף-בֵּית) after its first two letters. All 22 letters are consonants. Today's five are highlighted.

All 22 Letters
💡 Right to Left: Aleph (א) is #1. In a Hebrew text you begin reading from the top-right corner of the page and move left across each line.
Key Characteristics

No capital letters. Hebrew has no distinction between uppercase and lowercase. Five letters have a special final form when at the end of a word — you'll meet those later.

Consonants only (originally). Classical Hebrew was written with consonants only. The vowel pointing system (Niqqud) appears as dots and dashes and was added much later by the Masoretes.

Letters have numerical values. Aleph = 1, Bet = 2, and so on. This system is called Gematria.

Part 1 · Letters

Today's Five Letters

We learn the Hebrew alphabet in groups of five. Today: Aleph, Bet, Gimel, Dalet, He. Study each carefully.

Deep Dive
א Aleph — The Silent Letter

Aleph has no sound of its own. It acts as a carrier for vowels — the sound you pronounce is whatever vowel is attached to it. In ancient pictographic Hebrew, Aleph represented an ox — the first of animals, the beginning of all things.

In Scripture: The first word of the Bible — בְּרֵאשִׁית (Bereshit, "In the beginning") — begins with Bet, not Aleph. Yet the Ten Commandments begin with Anokhi (אָנֹכִי, "I am") — Aleph. God's first word to Israel is the silent letter made audible.
ב Bet / Vet — B or V

Bet makes a "B" sound with a dot inside (Dagesh): בּ. Without the dot it makes a "V" sound: ב. This is your first encounter with the dual-pronunciation principle. Bet means house — the letter originally looked like a floor plan of a dwelling.

Grammar: The prefix בְּ means "in" — so בְּרֵאשִׁית literally means "in-beginning."
ג Gimel — G as in "Good"

Gimel makes a hard "G" sound — never soft as in "gem." The ancient pictograph was a camel (Hebrew: gamal). Gimel is associated with gemilut chasadim — acts of loving kindness.

ד Dalet — D as in "Door"

Dalet makes a "D" sound. Its ancient form looked like a door (Hebrew: delet). Its shape — a right angle — resembles a doorway. Watch the difference between Dalet (ד) and Resh (ר) carefully — they look very similar.

ה He — H as in "Help"

He makes a soft "H" sound. It appears twice in God's covenant name: יהוה (YHWH). He is also the Hebrew definite article prefix — הַ means "the."

💡 Memory trick: Aleph = silent ox, Bet = house, Gimel = camel, Dalet = door, He = breath/window. Visualise the ancient picture for each letter.
Part 2 · Practice

Writing the Letters

Get a pen and paper. Writing by hand is the fastest way to cement shapes in memory. Write each letter 10 times following the guides below.

💡 You need: A notebook or blank paper and a pen. Hebrew is written right-to-left with a slight rightward slant. Don't worry about perfection — focus on the shape.
Stroke Guides
א Aleph
א
  • Draw a diagonal stroke from upper-right to lower-left (the main body)
  • Add a small Yod stroke at the upper left
  • Add a small Yod stroke at the lower right
  • Result: looks like an X with a diagonal spine
  • Write it 10 times.
ב Bet
ב
  • Draw a horizontal line across the top (right to left)
  • Drop a vertical stroke down on the right side
  • Draw a short foot at the bottom-left
  • Looks like an open box — a house without a roof on the left
  • Write it 10 times. Then add a dot for בּ (Bet with Dagesh).
ג Gimel
ג
  • Draw a short vertical stroke downward
  • At the bottom, curve right then bring a foot leftward
  • Think of a leg taking a stride — a camel's stride
  • Write it 10 times.
ד Dalet
ד
  • Draw a horizontal stroke across the top
  • Drop a straight vertical line down from the right end
  • Key: sharp right-angle corner — this distinguishes it from Resh (ר) which has a rounded top
  • Write Dalet and Resh side-by-side 5 times to see the difference.
ה He
ה
  • Same horizontal top and right vertical as Dalet
  • Add a short unattached vertical stroke on the left — it does NOT touch the top stroke
  • He = Dalet with an inner post — like a window inside a doorframe
  • Write He and Dalet side-by-side 5 times. The gap is the difference.
✍ Practice round: Write all five letters in order — א ב ג ד ה — right to left, 5 times across. Then close your eyes and try to write them from memory. Check your work.

Genesis 1:1 — The First Verse

Here is the first verse of the Bible in Hebrew. See if you can spot the letters you learned today — Aleph, Bet, Gimel, Dalet, and He:

בְּרֵאשִׁית בָּרָא אֱלֹהִים אֵת הַשָּׁמַיִם וְאֵת הָאָרֶץ
Bereshit bara Elohim et hashamayim ve'et ha'aretz
"In the beginning, God created the heavens and the earth."

Find: Bet (בּ) at the very start · Aleph (א) inside אֱלֹהִים · He (ה) in הַשָּׁמַיִם (the heavens) · Aleph in וְאֵת · He again in הָאָרֶץ (the earth).

💡 Meditate on this: The Hebrew word Davar (דָּבָר) — which you just learned — means both "word" and "thing." In Hebrew thought, a spoken word creates reality. When John writes "In the beginning was the Word," he is drawing a direct line to this Hebrew concept. You are beginning to see Scripture's depths.
